Our energy challenge: Revisiting assumptions about climate change

By Nikos Tsafos

Why is the world so slow to fight climate change?

Lots of reasons, of course.

But let's focus on one: a major assumption we make about energy, climate and the economy.

That you cannot grow the economy while reducing CO2 emissions.

It's hard to be optimistic about fighting climate change if you believe this.

Is this assumption true?

What if it's not?

Can we grow the economy and reduce CO2 emissions?

Richer countries use more energy, and thus, emit more CO2 emissions.

But the relationship is neither steady nor linear.

Countries at similar income levels have vastly different CO2 footprints.

Switzerland and Qatar. Similar incomes (~$75k per capita), but Qatar emits almost eight times more CO2 per capita than Switzerland.

Portugal and Bahrain. Similar incomes (~$22k per capita), but Bahrain emits almost four times more CO2 per capita than Portugal.

Panama and Curacao. Similar incomes (~$10-12k per capita), but Curacao emits eleven times more CO2 per capita than Panama.

Or: Switzerland and Portugal. Switzerland emits 12% more CO2, but its income is 3.5x higher.

Trinidad and Tobago and Luxembourg. Similar CO2 emissions (~16 tons per capita) but incomes are 6.4x higher in Luxembourg.

In short: Income per se is not a good predictor of CO2 emissions.

Incomes and CO2 emissions: Digging deeper

Why not?

First, countries use different amounts of energy.

Their economies may be different. More industry or more services.

Their climate may be different. More heating or more AC.

Their energy prices may be different. Expensive or cheap. Subsidized or heavily taxed.

Their capital may be different. Older buildings. Newer machines.

Their policies may be different. Stricter regulations. Tighter controls.

These differences are obvious in energy consumption patterns.

Malta and Bahrain. Similar incomes (~$20k per capita), but Bahrain consumes 5x more energy per capita as Malta.

Trinidad and Tobago and Uruguay. Similar incomes ($14-16K per capita), but Trinidad consumes 9x more energy per capita than Uruguay.

Once again, income is a poor predictor of energy consumption.

Second, countries generate their energy in different ways.

More fossil fuels such as coal, oil and gas mean more CO2 emissions than hydro, nuclear power, wind or solar.

Qatar and Iceland have similar energy use (20 toe vs 18 toe per capita), but CO2 emissions are 5x higher in Qatar.

Qatar uses oil and gas; Iceland uses hydro and geothermal energy.

Australia consumes slightly (7%) more energy per capita than Sweden, but its emissions are over 4x higher.

Germany's emissions are 2x higher than France's, even though they consume similar amounts of energy.

Not even energy consumption, on its own, predicts CO2 emissions.

Countries with similar incomes can have vastly different CO2 footprints.

Incomes and CO2 emissions: Trends over time

So far, we have looked only at 2014 data.

Maybe countries end up at different places.

But can an economy grow without more energy? Or more CO2 emissions?

The evidence is clear: yes.

But it is not a given.

There are so many different patterns.

Pattern #1 (Turkey): GDP, energy consumption and CO2 emissions are closely linked. Not perfectly overlapping, but close.

Pattern #2 (Australia): Income at some point delinks from energy use and CO2 emissions.

Pattern #3 (Russia): GDP is growing but energy use and emissions are not because there are still major efficiency gains to be had.

Pattern #4 (Germany): CO2 emissions falling faster than energy use; usually because countries relied more on gas or nuclear power.

Pattern #5 (United Kingdom): No clear link between GDP, energy and CO2 emissions: incomes grow faster than energy or emissions

Pattern #6 (China): Income, energy use and emissions grow, but not in tandem: incomes are up 10x in China (vs 1990), energy use 3x, and CO2 emissions 4x.

Pattern #7 (Iran): Energy use and CO2 emissions growing faster than incomes; energy-dependent growth models that do not deliver commensurate growth.

Pattern #8 (Bangladesh): CO2 emissions growing faster than energy use or incomes, Usually as countries replace traditonal (e.g. biomass) for oil, gas, and coal, which emit more CO2.

In sum: there is no reason to think that economic growth equals more energy consumption.

Or that more energy consumption must equal more CO2 emissions.

We have so many different trajectories around the world and throughout history.

The big picture: Living standards up; emissions less so

Let's step back and look at the big picture.

Most countries continue to grow: in 101 countries, real GDP per capita was either at its highest level or just below its peak (peak occurred in 2008-2013).

These are the countries shown in blue.

There are also a few countries where per capita GDP peaked before 1990.

That's because of war, revolution or economic stagnation, often from natural resources.

Finally, there are countries where per capita incomes have stagnated, peaking after 1990 but before 2007 (21 countries), many of them in Europe.

Per capita CO2 emissions show a dramatically different pattern.

In 30 countries, they peaked before 1990. This is in Europe, North America and parts of Africa (largely due to war).

In the Former Soviet Union, energy consumption and CO2 emissions have been declining since 1990, often at rapid rates.

Per capita emissions are rising mostly in Asia and Latin America, as well as parts of Africa (36 countries in total).

In all other places, emissions peaked either recently (25 countries in 2008-2013), or a long time ago.

What does all this mean?

We know that the link between incomes and CO2 emissions is far from linear.

We have so many countries that have grown without using more energy and/or emitting more CO2.

Not all countries are equal, of course: the growth in China and India can overwhelm what happens in many other countries.

Yet the broad picture is clear: so many countries where GDP per capita is growing while CO2 emissions are not.
